Inside UCLA's basketball facility, chess became a daily ritual — played on Poison Pawn boards between practices and film.

This spring, ESPN put a name to something that had been spreading through NBA locker rooms for years: the league's quiet chess boom. Its 2026 feature opens on Giannis Antetokounmpo stripping LeBron James on a late possession because he'd read the play before it happened — "I was just thinking ahead," Giannis said afterward. Victor Wembanyama has been taking on all comers in Washington Square Park. Derrick Rose just built an entire chess festival in Las Vegas alongside Magnus Carlsen. Per ChessBase, the roll call of stars who now champion the game runs through Wembanyama, Luka Dončić, Giannis and Draymond Green.

The reasons they give are remarkably consistent, and none is about becoming a coffeehouse champion. Anticipating a move. Recognizing patterns. Staying a step ahead. Staying calm when one decision decides everything. As Rose put it, you start to see how much a single move matters — and that one move is a choice in life. The board, it turns out, is just a faster, more honest version of the floor.

But there's a difference between playing chess and weaponizing it. For the league, the game is a hobby — a quieter way to compete, a phone app before tip-off. For one program, it's a discipline: a structured method for sharpening decisions under pressure, built into a training practice that has worked with Super Bowl MVPs, NBA champions and Olympic gold medalists. The NBA is discovering the game. Poison Pawn is the authority on turning it into an edge. To see what that actually looks like, rewind to a locker room in Westwood.

Westwood · February 2023

The day the system showed up in Westwood

It began, as these things do, with something small: a new chess set on the glass table in the lobby of UCLA's Mo Ostin Basketball Center. Nobody on the team had ordered it. It had been delivered by the founder of Poison Pawn — a crisp black-and-white board, a set of redesigned pieces — and it was less a gift than a delivery system for a method.

When Jaime Jaquez Jr. sat down nearby and noticed it, the basketball conversation was over. His eyes went wide. He wanted to know if the man who'd brought it was still around — the coach who trains some of the most elite athletes in the world to make better decisions under time and pressure, with chess as the tool. Jaquez wanted in. As The Athletic chronicled that spring, the Bruins were quietly becoming something stranger than a Pac-12 contender. They were becoming a chess team.

The System Underneath

Most players treat it as a hobby. Poison Pawn made it training.

Here is the distinction the whole story turns on. For most of the league, chess is a pastime — a quieter way to compete, a phone app before tip-off. Poison Pawn turned it into a method. Founded by Seth Makowsky and his wife, the program uses the game as a vehicle for cognitive training: a way to teach athletes to process faster, anticipate further, and decide cleanly under pressure. Makowsky — whom The Athletic has singled out as the most unusual quarterback guru in the game — has been a performance coach at UCLA since 2019, and the same chess-based system that ran through the football program, around quarterbacks like Jalen Hurts, C.J. Stroud and Joshua Dobbs, is what found its way onto those boards in the basketball facility.

The framework has a name. Makowsky teaches it as CDP — Control the Middle, Develop the Pieces, Protect the King — three chess principles that double as a decision-making operating system. It has traveled well beyond Westwood: Michigan State volleyball became the first collegiate women's program in the country to partner with Poison Pawn, and the client list runs through NFL quarterbacks, MVP-caliber baseball players, Olympic gold medalists and NBA champions. Where the rest of the league sees a hobby, Poison Pawn built a curriculum.

Inside Westwood

A team that played mind games

What made UCLA different wasn't that one or two players dabbled — it was that the habit took over the room. By the time the Bruins reached the 2023 NCAA Tournament, more of the team played than didn't. Every player had his own board; two community sets lived at the Ostin Center; injured guard Jaylen Clark closed out an online match against Jaquez moments before media availability, while Russell Stong finished off Tyger Campbell down the hall. Where other programs killed downtime on PlayStation, these guys played chess.

Ask them why, and the basketball logic poured out. Pressure situations. Finding the way out of a trap. Reading what an opponent intends and producing the counter. Playing four, five, six moves ahead — running a set not to score but to see how a defense reacts, then coming back with the answer. Clark described calling a play just to learn whether the big man would drop or roll, then attacking the read he'd uncovered. It is, almost exactly, how you stalk a position on the board.

Every move you make is your own.
— Jaime Jaquez Jr., on chess, to The Athletic

That was the part Jaquez loved most: total accountability, and the certainty that a solution always existed if you were disciplined enough to find it. You think you're stuck, he said — and then the position opens up. The answer was always there; you just had to find it before the clock did.

Westwood to South Beach

Jaquez carried it to the league

Jaquez is the clearest proof that this travels. On the Collegiate Chess Podcast, he talked about picking the game up alongside his teammates — and noted that the Poison Pawn founder already worked with much of the UCLA football team. "Seth Makowsky is a really cool dude, he's helping us as well," Jaquez said. He singled out one thing in particular as the part reshaping his game: the Blitz work with Makowsky, which he credited with helping him make good, quick decisions — and stick with them. Make the read, trust it, move.

It's like the game of life. There are so many reads, so many options... You have to constantly switch it up, adjust, and react to your opponent.
— Jaime Jaquez Jr., Collegiate Chess Podcast

Then he took it with him. As a Miami Heat rookie, Jaquez made the 2024 All-Rookie First Team, launched his own bot on Chess.com — joining Luka Dončić and Jaylen Brown — and was written up in the New Yorker for trading games with a teenage prodigy. He has said that playing chess before games gets him sharp and in the right mindset for the court. The habit that started on a borrowed board in a college lobby became part of how a professional prepares to compete.

The Through-Line

It was never about chess

Strip away the boards and the bots and the Washington Square Park crowds, and what the whole movement is really chasing is a single transferable skill: the ability to make a great decision when the moment is fast and the consequence is real. Anticipate. Read the trap. Commit. Protect the king. The reason it works on the hardwood is the same reason it works in a boardroom or a market — the board changes, the skill doesn't.

The NBA found chess. Poison Pawn built the method that turns it into an edge — the framework, the protocols, the discipline underneath the hobby. That's the part the spotlight is only now catching up to: not that elite athletes play the game, but that someone figured out how to make the game train them. The pieces were always on the board. The advantage is in knowing how to use them.

And the program at the heart of it isn't finished. This spring, UCLA's women cut down the nets for the school's first NCAA championship, led by senior Gabriela Jaquez — Jaime's younger sister. The same program that made chess part of its culture now houses champions on both sides of the building. The next chapter is already being written.
