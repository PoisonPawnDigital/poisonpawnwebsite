In a private conversation with Patricof Co's roster of athlete-investors, Poison Pawn founder Seth Makowsky made a quiet, radical case: the skill that separates champions from everyone else is the same one that separates great investors from broke ones — the discipline of the decision itself.

Featuring  Seth Makowsky Moderated by  Allie Barry, VP · Patricof Co Format  Recorded Conversation

There is a moment, in every game and every market, when the board tilts. The clock accelerates, the information thins, and the gap between the people who rise and the people who unravel turns out to have almost nothing to do with talent — and almost everything to do with the choice made in the next two seconds.

That tension is where Poison Pawn lives, and it's the premise Seth Makowsky carried into the Patricof Co Executive Speaker Series — the firm's invitation-only conversation series, recorded for its roster of professional-athlete clients and moderated by P/Co Vice President Allie Barry. It was a fitting room. The audience earned its first fortune on fields, courts and tracks. But they were sitting in it as something else now: investors, allocators, decision-makers with real capital and real consequences on the line.

The Room

A series built for people who decide for a living

Patricof Co — known across sports and finance simply as P/Co — is not a sports brand. It's a differentiated private investment and advisory platform that turns the privileged access of world-class athletes into a competitive edge in the deal market. Founded in late 2018 by Mark Patricof, son of venture-capital pioneer Alan Patricof, the firm co-invests alongside institutions like KKR, Bain and L Catterton, and its stated mission is to help athletes become independent-minded investors and high-performing business professionals.

The Unlikely Coach

He didn't come from sports. He came from the boardroom.

Here is the detail that makes Makowsky the right guest for an investment firm: he is not, by origin, a sports guy at all. The first college football game he ever attended, he was standing on the UCLA sideline as a coach. He was never a chess prodigy either. His background was running and growing companies — CEO of smaller organizations, then larger ones, turning them around — before he built his own advisory group taking on complex turnarounds for banks, airlines, airports, hotels and restaurant groups.

It was inside those rooms that the real lesson formed. "Just because you've got a great idea, that doesn't mean it works" — the hard part was always execution across a lot of decision-makers under pressure. In parallel, almost by accident, chess became an obsession. He went from studying with a grandmaster once a week to studying with grandmasters around the world — Russians approached the game one way, South Americans another — and set out to distill their jargon into something usable.

He became his own test subject. He started making faster decisions, growing comfortable with failure, treating everything as a process, and stepping off the emotional rollercoaster that quietly governs most perfectionists. Then, while running due diligence on an elite California sports academy, he noticed half the facility was devoted to the mental side of the game — pattern recognition, sequencing, coordinate training. It looked exactly like how he'd been studying chess. The academy's owner paired him with their best athletes to see if they'd break through the way he had. Olympic volleyball players and backup quarterbacks started breaking records. Word of mouth did the rest.

Even the company's name is a failure story. Before he ever trained an athlete, Makowsky and his wife set out to design the best chess sets in the world — and failed, by his count, hundreds of times. The failing was the point. It's where the comfort with risk was forged. They called the company Poison Pawn.

The Thesis

Everyone elite is in the same business

Chess, for Makowsky, is not the subject — it's the simulator. Within the first four moves of a game, he notes, there are more than 288 billion possible positions. That overwhelming complexity is exactly the point: it forces process thinking. If they go here, we do this. If this happens, we go there. A framework for deciding, rehearsed thousands of times in a small window, instead of waiting for the real moment to arrive once.

And the engine underneath it is brutally simple. In a chess game, if you take too long to decide, you lose — really lose, on the board, right now. Elite competitors hate losing, so they rewire their behavior fast. That's the whole mechanism: not a lecture about decisions, but a consequence that modifies them.

At the end of the day, you're in the decision-making business. If you make really, really good decisions, you're going to win.
— Seth Makowsky

That's why he'll cross-train an NFL quarterback alongside the head of the U.S. women's synchronized swimming program alongside a wide receiver — different sports, identical muscle. At the top, he argues, the physical gifts are table stakes. Every quarterback in an NFL room can throw; every player in a pro tennis draw can hit. What's left to separate them isn't physical at all.

Under Pressure

Two engines: the will to win, the fear of losing

Speed matters, Makowsky says — but it isn't the separator, and there's no single right way to decide. There are personality types. The impulsive decider moves fast and sometimes recklessly. The perfectionist overthinker won't move until the risk is zero, and so moves too late. The work is making tiny adjustments to each: getting the overthinker comfortable deciding faster, giving the impulsive decider a beat of process before instinct fires. Tiny adjustments that unlock massive results — like a quarterback going from the most turnovers in the league one season to the fewest the next.

But press him on the single biggest separator under pressure and the answer turns almost philosophical: the enthusiasm to win versus the fear of failure. They can look identical from the outside — same sweat, same stakes — yet they produce opposite decisions. One reaches; one flinches. And the great ones accept, in advance, that losing is part of it. Injury is part of it. Some coaches will like you, some won't. The job is to execute a process at a high level and refuse to spend energy on the rollercoaster.

The Method

To slow the game down, you train faster than the game

A quarterback might have three seconds before a very expensive, very motivated defender arrives. So Makowsky trains him to decide in under one. Sweating, under noise, under an athletic protocol that mimics the real chaos and then turns it up — so that three seconds, on game day, feels like an eternity. You can't lecture your way to it any more than you can read your way to a heavier deadlift.

You can study weightlifting. You can take the class. But at some point you have to lift the weight if you want to get stronger.
— Seth Makowsky

The brain, in his model, is a muscle — and it strengthens the same way the body does: under load, with reps, with discomfort. Just like an athlete trains their body, we use the same protocols to train their minds. The chessboard is brutally honest about whether the work is happening. How someone decides on the board is exactly how they decide on the field — ego, patience, composure, all of it surfaces — and because the result is objective, players self-correct. They don't want to lose. So they fix it themselves, and carry the fix onto the field.

Adversity

The injury that makes a better player

When adversity hits — injury, doubt, a cold streak — Makowsky starts by naming the trap. For an athlete whose identity is fused to the team, injury can be a dark, lonely room; the calls stop, and the self goes quiet. That, he says, is average. The pivot is immediate: if we can't use the body right now, how do we get the mind sharper? Faster decisions, better reads, more empathy as a leader. Poison Pawn even builds vision boards with athletes for the specific recovery window, porting the chess discipline onto that stage of life.

He points to a quarterback few had heard of — not a top prospect — who got hurt, and instead of disappearing, sat in every coaches' meeting and learned the playbook from the coach's chair. He came back, broke his school's passing record, was treated by his coach as an extra coach on staff, and made the NFL. Ask him today and he'll tell you the injury made him better.

Elite is taking anything that comes your way and using it to your advantage. So how do you compete with someone who's using their injury to get better?
— Seth Makowsky
The Investor's Lens

The same question a backer asks a founder

Here the conversation turned, and the bridge to finance came not from the coach but from the investor. As Makowsky described how he reads an athlete under pressure, Allie Barry recognized her own job. It was, she said, almost exactly what P/Co weighs when it sits across from a founder.

Is this founder tough enough mentally? If they take a dip in sales, are they going to be able to make a comeback — to put that play behind them and keep growing the company?
— Allie Barry, Vice President, Patricof Co

When a company faces headwinds — a soft economy, a missed quarter — the diligence question isn't only the spreadsheet. It's whether the person at the top can absorb the setback and still lead through it. Resilience, composure, the refusal to let one bad stretch snowball: the traits Makowsky trains into competitors are the same traits capital looks for before it commits. Different arena, identical read.

Beyond the Game

The competitive drive was never about the sport

The hardest pivot any athlete faces is the one P/Co exists to help with: what happens when the playing days end. Makowsky's answer is about identity. The discipline and drive an athlete carries dwarfs what most people ever develop — but it has to be transferred consciously, or even winning rings hollow. You sacrifice everything, you get the gold medal, and you wonder why it feels empty.

He pointed to an Olympic gold-medal swimmer he trains, Michael Andrew, who won't skip a beat when his career ends — because he already knows that he doesn't swim for the medals. He swims because he loves the water. When he's not swimming he's surfing; the purpose runs deeper than any trophy, and he's built a life around it (his next chapter, Makowsky noted, is diving for sunken treasure with his father, who is also his coach). The lesson is to find — as early as possible — what you actually love about the thing, and to define yourself as bigger than the job.

It is, Makowsky admits, a lesson he learned the hard way himself. After years as the CEO of a large organization, striking out on his own meant losing the title — the soul-searching, lonely stretch he now calls the best thing he ever did. Which is precisely the leap P/Co's clients are being asked to make: from being the asset to owning them. From player to principal.

The One Principle

The zero multiplier

Asked for the single idea every listener should carry out of the room, Makowsky didn't reach for motivation. He reached for risk management. In chess, rule number one is to protect the king. It doesn't matter how brilliantly you're playing or how far ahead you are — leave the king exposed for one move and the whole game ends. He trains it into players until they stop losing it. But, he tells them at the end of a session, this was never really about chess.

You can be crushing it on the field, crushing it in life, and make one genuinely bad decision off it — get behind the wheel after too many drinks — and it's a zero multiplier. Every rep, every sacrifice, every disciplined year goes to zero instantly. The traps have always been there and always will be; the discipline is to see them and take them off the table. It's the most important thing in chess, and the most important thing in life.

It is also, almost word for word, the first law of investing. Markets don't ruin you for the deals you miss; they ruin you for the position that wipes you out. A portfolio that touches zero stays at zero, no matter how brilliant every prior decision was. Capital preservation, position sizing, refusing the catastrophic single bet — this is how serious investors survive long enough to compound. As Barry put it, it lands for anyone: whatever you've spent your life building, why risk all of it for one avoidable mistake? The board changes. The rule doesn't.

Poison Pawn isn't built around a sport. It's built around the moment that actually determines outcomes — when time is tight, pressure is high, and the right call is worth everything. That moment shows up the same way on a field, in a boardroom and on a trading screen. Rewire how you decide, and the edge follows you everywhere. It takes the same work as the body. Not hard, not easy — just necessary.
